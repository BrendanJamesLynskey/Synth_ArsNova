About the Ars Nova

Mature Gothic polyphony • 14th century • France

The Ars Nova — the "new art" — takes its name from a treatise associated with Philippe de Vitry (c. 1322). It broke from the older Ars Antiqua by notating duple ("imperfect") time as the equal of triple, and by granting rhythm a freedom and precision it had never had. Its towering figure is Guillaume de Machaut, poet-composer of ballades, rondeaux, motets, and the first complete polyphonic setting of the Mass ordinary by one hand — the Messe de Nostre Dame.

Its signature device is isorhythm: a repeating rhythmic pattern — the talea — laid over a repeating series of pitches — the color. When the two are of different lengths they slip out of phase and realign only after many statements, generating vast, mathematically-scaffolded structures. The motet was sung, so this engine voices the faster upper parts (cantus, contratenor, triplum) with the shared FOF vocal-synthesis library (vocal-voices.js, the IRCAM CHANT method) — overlapping formant grains sung as Latin vowels — held over a slow, sustained instrumental tenor carrying the isorhythmic cantus firmus.

Where it sits in the lineage

The Ars Nova is the flowering of the polyphony first raised at Notre Dame. Plainsong gave the single line; organum added a second voice; the Ars Nova organised those voices into rhythmically independent parts over an isorhythmic tenor drawn from chant. From here the road runs on to the Renaissance. A parallel secular branch runs through the troubadours to instrumental dance.
